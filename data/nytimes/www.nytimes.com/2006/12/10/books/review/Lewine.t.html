    Sunday Book Review

    True Story

    By EDWARD LEWINEDEC. 10, 2006

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    No wonder John Grisham got excited when he found Ronald Williamson’s obituary in the pages of The New York Times. Here was a real person whose life read like one of Grisham’s best-selling legal thrillers: falsely accused of murder in the South, he was railroaded onto death row, only to be dramatically exonerated. Better yet, for the baseball-obsessed Grisham, Williamson had played in the minor leagues.

    Within hours of seeing the obituary, Grisham resolved to write a nonfiction book — his first — about the Williamson case. “Not in my most creative moment,” he explains, “could I conjure up a story as rich and as layered.”

    I think he’s selling himself short. Grisham isn’t the sort of writer whom Swedish foundations give big awards to, but he is a major talent. (If you think it’s easy writing wildly popular courtroom dramas, then you try it.) In “The Innocent Man,” however, he has shackled himself to facts that are less intriguing than he imagines, and he fails to use his creative gifts to help matters along.

    Ron Williamson was raised in tiny Ada, Okla. Drafted by the Oakland A’s in 1971, he was stymied by injuries and returned home to a life of drugs, drink and escalating mental illness. Then things got worse. Williamson and a pal named Dennis Fritz were arrested and in 1988 tried separately for the brutal 1982 killing of a local cocktail waitress, Debbie Sue Carter.

    In Grisham’s account both men are obviously innocent, but the legal system is so dimwitted and rigged against them that conviction is inevitable. Fritz received a sentence of life in prison; Williamson, death. More than a decade in Oklahoma’s medieval prisons followed — Williamson came within days of lethal injection — before they were freed by heroic lawyers using DNA evidence.

    This is an interesting and important tale, and also a familiar one. The first inmate was freed by DNA evidence some 17 years ago, and similar cases have been well covered since. There’s even a shortish account of Williamson’s ordeal in “Actual Innocence” (2000), written by Barry Scheck and Peter Neufeld — who represented Fritz and assisted Williamson’s lawyer during their DNA appeal — along with Jim Dwyer (then of The Daily News, now of The Times).

    “The Innocent Man” begins with a spare description of a rural village jolted by homicide that closely resembles the opening paragraphs of “In Cold Blood.” But while Grisham summons Truman’s ghost, he doesn’t pull off the Capote feat of investing a drab Middle American murder with novelistic heft.

    Some say Capote did this by making stuff up. If so, that’s a terrible strategy for the author of a book purporting to be nonfiction. Still, after reading “The Innocent Man,” you do wish Grisham had used some of his novelist’s technique to marshal the facts at his disposal.

    Instead, he never gets the plot up to a decent boil. Rather than focusing tightly on Williamson, he meanders through Fritz’s trial and yet a third murder case, whose details are instructive but not enough to merit the damage all this digressing does to the book’s forward motion. With so many crimes and trials, the cast swells to an unhealthy number, and few of these characters emerge as fully drawn people.

    It’s true in some cosmic sense that the story of every life has value, but not to the writer of nonfiction. Writers of nonfiction narratives learn to pick their subjects with care, because some true stories are much, much more interesting than others. In this case, John Grisham could have conjured up a better story on his own.

    Edward Lewine is the author of “Death and the Sun: A Matador’s Season in the Heart of Spain.”
